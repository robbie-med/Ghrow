How this app works

The app is static. It loads a curve catalog from data/catalog.json, fetches the selected CSV file, plots percentile columns from that file, and uses LMS values in the same rows to estimate Z-scores and percentiles for entered measurements.

Saved patient measurements are stored only in the browser. The data files are separate from the JavaScript so the raw source can be inspected, replaced, versioned, and cited.

Clinical caution: this is not a diagnostic device. Use corrected age, curve choice, gestational-age-specific curves, and local policy carefully.

Special populations — when to use which reference

The plotter ships with the WHO 2006, CDC 2000, and CDC infant references. The condition- and population-specific references below are documented here so the right chart can be chosen for the situation. The notes are general guidance — always consult the primary publication and local policy.

Fenton 2013

data not yet integrated

When to use: preterm infants from ~22 to 50 weeks postmenstrual (corrected) age — in the NICU and after discharge — for weight, length, and head circumference. Transition to WHO 2006 once the infant reaches term-corrected age.

Best for: very-low-birth-weight and preterm infants, where term age-based curves do not apply.

Source: Fenton TR, Kim JH. BMC Pediatrics 2013;13:59.

INTERGROWTH-21st

data not yet integrated

When to use: prescriptive international standards. Newborn Size (size-for-gestational-age at birth, 24–42+ weeks) classifies SGA / AGA / LGA; the Preterm Postnatal Growth standards are an alternative to Fenton; the term 0–5y standards extend the WHO approach.

Best for: size-for-gestational-age at birth, preterm postnatal growth, and settings that prefer a single international prescriptive standard.

Source: Villar J, et al. Lancet 2014; INTERGROWTH-21st Project.

China (national reference)

data not yet integrated

When to use: children in China or of Chinese descent, birth–18 years, where a national population reference reflects the population better than WHO/CDC.

Best for: population-specific monitoring where local references are standard of care.

Source: Capital Institute of Pediatrics; national growth surveys of Chinese children.

Down syndrome

data not yet integrated

When to use: children with Trisomy 21, birth–20 years. The AAP recommends plotting on standard WHO/CDC charts and Down-syndrome-specific charts, which account for shorter stature and different weight/BMI trajectories.

Best for: condition-specific surveillance alongside the standard charts.

Source: Zemel BS, et al. Pediatrics 2015;136:e1204 (US Down syndrome growth charts).
